# Choosing canned whole tomatoes

When the goal is a smooth, saucy result from whole peeled tomatoes (blitzed or hand-mashed, then simmered), the deciding factor is **calcium chloride**, a firming agent. It keeps tomatoes intact and stops them breaking down during cooking. Read the ingredient list, not the front of the can.

- **For sauce (mash/blitz → simmer): pick cans with NO calcium chloride.** Genuine Italian San Marzano DOP cans are barred from containing it and collapse into a silky sauce — e.g. Cento San Marzano *Certified*, Pastene DOP. These taste mushy/metallic raw but excellent once cooked, which is the right use case here.
- **For chunky texture (brothy stews, chunky pasta): cans WITH calcium chloride are the better tool, not a compromise.** Most domestic brands (Red Gold, regular Muir Glen, Hunt's whole plum) include it and hold firm pieces through a simmer.
- **"San Marzano–style" on a domestic can is mostly marketing.** It doesn't reliably mean DOP quality and most still carry calcium chloride. Judge by the ingredient list.

Note on the saved brand list: Cento's *Certified San Marzano* line fits the sauce use; Muir Glen (regular and "San Marzano Style") both contain calcium chloride; DeLallo varies by product (true DOP vs domestic), so check the specific can.
